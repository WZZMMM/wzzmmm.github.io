Cole and Fernando (2021, EJ)

Cole, Shawn A, and A Nilesh Fernando. 2021. “‘Mobile’Izing Agricultural Advice Technology Adoption Diffusion and Sustainability.” The Economic Journal 131 (633): 192–219. https://doi.org/10.1093/ej/ueaa084.

Author(s)

  • Cole: Shawn A. Cole - John G. McLean Professor in the Finance Unit at Harvard Business School
  • Fernando: A. Nilesh Fernando

Research Question

  • Does a voice-based advisory service via mobile phone facilitate changes in technology adoption by farmers?
  • Considering this innovative extension, what's different from in-person extension, and what's similar to? (e.g. the peer effect)

Data

  • RCT data from 1,200 cotton-farming households in Gujarat, India.
  • Server logs from the Avaaj Otalo (AO) platform
  • A ‘peer survey’ to collect more information from 1,114 non-study respondents

Method

  • RCT: the research team randomly assigned toll-free access to AO to 400 households (hereafter, ‘AO group’) and to test the hypothesis that ICT would not be effective without at least some in-person element, an additional 400 households received both AO and an annual in-person extension session (hereafter, ‘AOE group’). A further 400 households served as a pure control group.
  • ANCOVA specification as suggested by McKenzie (2012, JDE) to increase our power to detect effects, given the low autocorrelation of most outcomes in our data.
  • ‘peer survey’ and ‘spillover’ speciation: we asked all respondents to list the three contacts with whom they most frequently discussed agricultural information. As such, we are able to identify whether any of these peers also received the treatment
  • we adopt the double LASSO machine learning approach (DML) to pick an optimal set of control variables
  • Measuring WTP: We visited the 1,200 study respondents, as well as an additional 457 non-study respondents. One-quarter of this sample (chosen at random, via scratch cards) was offered a fixed price for a nine-month subscription at a single price (‘take it or leave it’ offer, or TIOLI); we randomly varied that price across farmers to estimate demand.33 For the remaining three-quarters of the sample, we used a modified version of the BDM method (Becker-Degroot-Marschak, 1964 Behav. Sci.) to elicit WTP.

Contribution

  • Evaluation of voice-based extension.
  • Rare evidence on WTP for digital advisory services and links usage to behavioral changes despite null effects on yields.
  • Method to specificize spillovers in household-level RCTs and tests complementarity between two services (mobile-phone-based and in-person extension).

Core Ref

  • A large literature focuses on the microeconomics of technology adoption (for a survey, see Foster and Rosenzweig, 2010).
  • This work complements a developing literature on the potential for digital agricultural extension (see Fabregas et al., 2019 for a recent review).

Results

  • High demand & trust: 90% of treated farmers used AO (avg. 7 hours), 40% asked questions. Trust in mobile advice rose by 5.8 points (10-point scale).
  • Behavioral change: 5.8 SD increase in mobile-based info usage index; reduced reliance on input dealers for pesticide advice. Cotton management index rose by 0.13–0.15 SD, driven by seed choices (e.g., +6 pp for recommended Bt variety).
  • No yield/profit gains: Null effects on cotton, wheat, and cumin yields (point estimates noisy and insignificant). Input expenditure rose by 8% (15% for irrigation, 20% for seeds).
  • WTP & Spillovers: By BDM method, average WTP = $1.79 (Rs. 109). Treated farmers with more treated peers used AO more intensively. Non-study peers of treated farmers reported 4% lower pest-related crop losses.

Conclusion

  • The RCT intervention was successful in generating a substantial usage of AO, a mobile phone-based technology that allows farmers to receive timely agricultural information from expert agronomists and their peers.
  • Advice provided through AO resulted in farmers consistently changing their input decisions.
  • "We do not find evidence to suggest the service improved yields or profits. Unfortunately, the imprecision of our estimates suggests the study was underpowered to detect effects for these outcomes, suggesting a need for future research. "

Research Question

  • What's the impacts of text message–based agricultural extension programs on individuals’ decisions to acquire recommended inputs?
  • What's the different impacts by different contexts / data sources?

Data

  • 6 text message–based agricultural extension (in Kenya and Rwanda by 3 different organizations). In total, over 128,000 individuals participated across all six experiments.

Method

  • RCT

  • Meta-analysis: odds ratios (see II.B and )

    We use a random-effects model, which assumes that true effects can vary across studies and are normally distributed. The weighted average effect, therefore, represents the mean of the distribution of true effects. Formally, the model can be written as

    (2)Tj=μ+ej+ζj,

    where Tj is the observed effect for study j,μ is the underlying true average effect, ej represents the measurement error due to sampling variation, and ζj is the difference between the average effect, and the effect of program j . Moreover, ej∼ N(0,σj2) and ζj∼N(0,τ2) . σj2 is the within-study variance for study j , while τ2 is the between-study variance. The estimate of μ is

    (3)μ^=∑j=1swjTj∑j=1swj,

    where wj are study-specific weights given by the inverse of the variance and s is the number of studies. In this case,

    (4)wj=1(τ^2+σ^j2)

    and in practice, we estimate τ2 using the DerSimonian and Laird method (DerSimonian and Laird 1986). We conduct robustness checks using a number of alternative estimation methods (Sidik-Jonkman, restricted maximum likelihood, and empirical Bayes). In addition to τ2 , we report two other measures of heterogeneity across programs: Cochran’s Q -statistic to test the null hypothesis of homogeneous effects across studies and Higgin’s and Thompson’s I2 , which is the percentage of variability not explained by sampling error (Higgins et al. 2003; Higgins and Thompson 2002).

Contribution

  • We present evidence of programs with substantial sample sizes.
  • Across all experiments, we use actual input acquisitions as our primary measure of behavior change .
  • A meta-analysis over the experiments.

Core Ref

  • Why the empirical evidence on the impacts of digital agricultural extension programs on recipient behavior has been characterized as mixed:

    1. program effectiveness is very sensitive to the specific features of its design (Pritchett and Sandefur 2015)
    2. sampling variation (Meager 2019)
    3. selection biases (Glewwe et al. 2004)
    4. varying levels of statistical power (Ioannidis, Stanley, and Doucouliagos 2017)
    5. differences in measurement, or publication biases (DellaVigna and Linos 2022)

Results

  • Meta-analysis results

    • Overall effect on following recommended inputs (lime + recommended fertilizers): odds ratio (OR) = 1.22 (95% CI 1.16–1.29; N = 6). Linear-probability (absolute) meta-effect ≈ +2 percentage points (95% CI 0.01–0.03).
    • Lime-specific: OR = 1.19 (95% CI 1.11–1.27). Quantity meta-effect ≈ +1.18 kg lime (wide heterogeneity).
    • Fertilizer-specific (four programs that recommended fertilizers): OR = 1.27 (95% CI 1.15–1.40). Absolute change ≈ +1 percentage point (LPM).
    • Purchased any fertilizer (overall purchases): OR ≈ 1.16 (95% CI 0.94–1.42) — smaller and not statistically significant, suggesting substitution across fertilizer types.

  • Lessons from individual experiments

    • Differential Effects of Self-Reported versus Administrative Data: Effects estimated from endline surveys tend to be larger than effects measured using administrative purchase records.
    • Effects of Message Framing, Content, and Repetition: Different message framings and added specificity generally did not change purchase behavior relative to simple messages, while message repetition produced modest positive increases in purchases.
    • Are Text Messages Strengthened by Phone Calls? Adding phone calls increased awareness but did not yield significant additional increases in following recommendations or purchases relative to SMS-only, and take-up of offered calls was low.
    • Are There Spillovers? In the cleaner two-stage 1AF design there is suggestive evidence that untreated peers in partially treated groups experienced small positive spillovers to lime adoption, but spillover evidence is not uniformly strong across all specifications and projects.

Conclusion

  • The results highlight the importance of well-powered experiments, especially for very cheap interventions, and caution against making conclusions about the external validity of programs by simply taking nonsignificant results as evidence of no impact.

Comments

  • Context matters in RCT-based researches.
  • The research looks more abundant, rigorous and credible due to substantial sample sizes (actually, sufficient funding).
